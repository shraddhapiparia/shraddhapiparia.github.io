Living With “Future Shock” in Research

Published 14 July 2025

A colleague once suggested I read Future Shock by Alvin Toffler. Written in 1970, the book centers on a simple observation: rapid change can overwhelm people and institutions before they have time to adapt.

Research environments make this especially visible. Methods evolve, tools are replaced, and entire subfields can shift direction within a few years. Stability is the exception rather than the norm.

Change as a working condition

Rather than treating change as an occasional disruption, it may be more accurate to see it as a persistent condition of research work. Projects, collaborations, and even expertise can become outdated faster than expected.

This does not necessarily imply failure. It reflects the pace at which knowledge and technology move.

Practices that seem to help

Over time, researchers develop informal strategies for operating under these conditions. None are guarantees, but some patterns recur:

  • Allocating regular time to survey new tools and ideas, without feeling compelled to adopt all of them.
  • Accepting that not every project reaches a traditional endpoint, and that partial results can still be shared.
  • Being selective about novelty, distinguishing between methods that clarify a question and those that merely attract attention.
  • Working across disciplines, which can broaden perspective when familiar approaches stall.
  • Maintaining summaries, notes, and peer conversations that help reconnect day-to-day work with broader goals.
  • Continuing to reskill while recognizing that no skill set remains sufficient indefinitely.

These are not prescriptions so much as observations about how researchers adapt over time.

A reframing, not a solution

Change in research is unlikely to slow down. Treating adaptability as an ongoing practice rather than a one-time adjustment can make this reality easier to work with.

In that sense, “future shock” is less a problem to solve than a condition to navigate—one that shapes how research is planned, evaluated, and sustained.
