What Biology Taught Me About Change

I used to think of biology mostly as something we inherit.

You are born with a particular genome, a particular family history, a particular set of risks. It is easy to hear words like “genetic predisposition” and feel that the story is already written.

The more I learned biology, the less I believed that.

Genes matter, but they are not the whole system.

The genome provides potential. Regulation determines which parts of that potential become active. Expression changes over time. Proteins, pathways, stress, sleep, exercise, diet, and environment all push the system in different directions.

Biology behaves less like a fixed blueprint and more like a control system with feedback loops. The same genome can produce very different outcomes depending on which signals are repeatedly reinforced.

I find this idea strangely hopeful.

A family history is not a sentence. A predisposition is not a destiny.

For common conditions such as type 2 diabetes, heart disease, obesity, anxiety, and depression, genes often create vulnerability, but daily habits shape how strongly that vulnerability appears.

People often talk about genetics as though there is a single “gene for” a disease. In reality, many common diseases emerge from hundreds or thousands of small changes interacting across layers:

  • genetic variants
  • stress and environment
  • inflammation
  • sleep
  • exercise
  • diet
  • social connection
  • repeated patterns of thought and behavior

Over time, those small changes accumulate. They can move the system toward disease, but they can also move it away from disease.

Exercise is one of the clearest examples. It does not change your DNA sequence, but it changes the system built on top of it. Exercise affects inflammation, insulin sensitivity, hormone signaling, stress pathways, sleep, metabolism, and even gene expression. A person may carry genetic risk for diabetes or cardiovascular disease and still reduce that risk substantially through movement and routine.

The same is true for food. Better nutrition does not “erase” genes, but it changes the pathways through which those genes act. The body becomes less inflamed, metabolic signals become more stable, and the system is less likely to drift toward disease.

Along the way, one resource that influenced me was Andrew Huberman’s podcast. It connected many ideas that had previously felt separate to me: sleep, exercise, stress, light, food, focus, and habit formation. It helped me think about lifestyle not as a list of rules, but as a set of repeated signals that gradually shape the system.

Meditation changed the way I think about this most of all.

After a meditation retreat, I did not feel like I had become a different person. I felt like different pathways had become easier to access. My mind felt less reactive and more stable. The same thoughts still existed, but the system responded differently.

Biology gave me a language for that experience.

We are not fixed. We are dynamic systems.

The hopeful part is that the same is true in the other direction.

Modern life also brings modern challenges. We sit more, sleep less, eat more processed food, experience more chronic stress, and spend more time disconnected from routine and community than many previous generations. These pressures can gradually push the system in the wrong direction.

But awareness matters. Understanding how these factors affect biology gives us tools to work with the system rather than against it. We may not control everything, but small, consistent changes can go a long way.

A walk every day. Better sleep. More exercise. Healthier food. Meditation. Less stress. More consistency.

These are not restrictions, and there is no single correct way to eat, exercise, or live. Biology is not improved by becoming rigid or stressed about doing everything perfectly. In many cases, the stress of constantly trying to optimize can become part of the problem.

What matters more is building habits that are sustainable and supportive for you.

None of these changes are dramatic in a single day. But biology is not controlled by one dramatic event. It is shaped by repeated signals.

The lesson I take from biology is not that we are trapped by our genes.

It is that we are always interacting with them.
