DNA Sequencing Explained: What Does Reading the Genome Mean?

This article is part of the Foundations of Genomic Data series.

What does DNA sequencing capture?

DNA sequencing is the process of reading the order of nucleotides along the genome. Conceptually, it provides access to the complete genetic code an individual carries.

Importantly, sequencing does not selectively read genes. It reads whatever DNA is present in the sampled material. This includes protein-coding regions, noncoding regions, regulatory elements, and repetitive sequences.

As a result, DNA sequencing captures potential biological variation rather than biological activity.

Most of the genome is not protein-coding

A common misconception is that DNA sequencing is primarily about genes. In reality, protein-coding regions account for only a small fraction of the genome.

This matters because DNA sequencing inherently measures far more than gene-coding changes. Many variants identified through sequencing lie outside exons and influence biology indirectly, through regulation or genome structure.

Types of DNA sequencing

DNA sequencing is applied at different scopes depending on the scientific question.

Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS) reads nearly the entire genome. It provides the most comprehensive view of genetic variation, including noncoding and structural variants.

Whole Exome Sequencing (WES) targets only protein-coding regions. While more limited, it is efficient for studies focused on coding mutations, particularly in rare disease settings.

Targeted sequencing focuses on predefined genomic regions. This approach is useful when prior biological knowledge narrows the search space.

Each strategy reflects a tradeoff between coverage, cost, and interpretability.

What DNA sequencing is good at

DNA sequencing is particularly well suited for identifying inherited genetic variation. Common applications include:

Discovery of single-nucleotide variants and small insertions or deletions.

Detection of structural variants.

Characterization of rare mutations.

Population-level genetic analysis.

Because DNA is largely stable across cells, sequencing provides a consistent reference layer that supports downstream analyses.

What DNA sequencing does not measure

DNA sequencing does not indicate whether a gene is active, suppressed, or differentially expressed. Two individuals with identical DNA sequences may show very different biological behavior at the RNA or protein level.

This limitation is not a flaw; it reflects the biological role of DNA itself. Sequencing reveals the genetic substrate, not its real-time execution.

Why sequencing is only one part of the picture

DNA sequencing explains what genetic variation exists, but not how that variation manifests in cells or tissues. For this reason, sequencing is often complemented by RNA-based measurements and other molecular assays.
